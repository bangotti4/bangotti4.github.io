The Data Science Pipeline Through Casual Gaming:¶

-By Ben Angotti

This tutorial is meant to serve as a guide through a Data Scientist's typical exploration of a question and the data used to answer it. Though the work can take many forms and often need repeat steps to fully understand the data, the general flow of tasks can be listed in the following order:

  1. Data collection/curation + parsing
  2. Data management/representation
  3. Exploratory data analysis
  4. Hypothesis testing and machine learning
  5. Communication of insights attained

Known as the "Data Science Pipeline", these steps are a general explanation of how Data Scientists research and analyze problems.

Introduction and Context¶

One of the most popular forms of media in the present age is online gaming. As such, many websites have come to devote themselves to games and their statistics. For any given game, you can find a dozen websites with facts and statistics on every aspect of it, with one of the most popular forms of website being online tier lists. Any game with some sort of competitive nature will develop a tier list for the games various choises, whether it be characters, items, decisions, or any other factor.

League of Legends has grown into an international phenemenon and has numerous sources of data available to aspiring data scientists. As such, dozens of websites have dedicated themselves to gathering and displaying an incredible amount of data to show character (referred to as "champions") viability. This data is often compiled into large tables of numbers and names that are useful for finding single bits of information at a time, but difficult to examine all 150+ champions to search for trends and interesting patterns. This is where the Data Science Pipeline comes in. Using various tools already prepared for use in data analysis, we can extract this data and visualize it in such a way that we can reach new conclusions.

For the sake of this tutorial, I show how to go through each step of the Data Science Pipeline using my personal favorite gamemode, ARAM. ARAM is short for "All Random All Mid", and features a map with a single bridge on which two 5-person teams attempt to capture the others territory. Each team is given a small amount of randomly chosen champions to choose from, giving players a small amount of agency before the game's start.

The next step of cleanup is a bit tricky to spot at first. Each column is stored as its own datatype and therefore needs to be assigned accordingly. Numerical columns, such as "Pick %" and "Win %" can sometimes contain non-numeric symbols. In this case, they contain the "%" symbol, and must be cast to numerical values. Even the "KDA" column is currently not labeled as numeric, because our code believes the decimal point to be a character instead of a numerical seperator.

It is important to note that the Data Science Pipeline is not always a linear track through science. Often times, the scientist will have to backtrack and repeat a step or two in order to progress forward. Sometimes more data is needed, and so they temporarily return to the first step to gather more. In the case of this tutorial, I had failed to notice that the integers were not stored as such until I attempted to graph them. Graphing non-integers led to some unfortunate looking graphs, so I had to revisit the cleanup stage. For the sake of clarity, I have grouped all aspects of cleanup together, but be aware that you will often not notice all the details about your data when you first receive it.

As an aside, I am leaving the "Score" and "Trend" columns alone for now, as they are useful for reference, but not needed for visualization at this time.

It is also worth noting that the "Secondary" column features a number of missing values (listed as NaN). This is because some champions only fulfil one role (Purely a mage, or purely support). We would describe this as "Data Missing Not At Random", or MNAR, because we are aware of exactly why the data is missing. Typically, we would have to decide whether or not to involve data with missing entries in our evaluation, or come up with a model to fill the gaps, but since we are aware of why the data is missing, we can instead use it later down the line.

Step 3 - Exploratory Data Analysis (Visualization)¶

Once we have reached a point where we believe our data is clean enough to be explored, we can start looking for interesting trends. Before proceeding however, it is important to clarify what trend we are actually looking to identify. This will give us direction when we examine the resulting graphs and output, and often requires some insight into the topic at hand. In the case of ARAM in League of Legends, I have accumulated some personal experience that has led to ideas I have had about the gamemode. My desire to confirm or disprove these ideas is what led me to choose this topic for exploration, and drives the data Science Pipeline as a whole.

ARAM has a couple of additional rules outside of its semi-randomness and interesting map design (being a single lane with teams on either side). The player is not allowed to return to their base and heal up battle wounds. In addition, the player is only able to acquire additional items after dying. This leads to a game flow where the player is encouraged to take care of their Health Points and choose moments of battle wisely. For this reason and the lack of space to maneuver due to map design, I believe Mages are the superior champion type to use in ARAM. Mages often have spells that cover a large area, or have the range to cast them from a safe distance. This allows them to slowly build an advantage against the enemy team, and take fights once they feel they can easily win them, and therefore see better performance numbers.

In addition to my own hypothesis, there is also the Null-Hypothesis, which would state that there is no significant statistical difference between populations. In this case, the Null-Hypothesis would suggest that there would be no significant performance difference between champion type.

Finally, we should also see if there are any discernable relationships between our major three columns: Win %, Pick %, and KDA. If any trends are present, we may able to build a model to predict champion performance.

The first graph I decided to check was to see if champions that were picked more often were more successful than those who were not. For context, despite ARAM being "all random", the actual process allows players to select a champion from a select few random options. For the sake of examination, we will operate under the assumption that each champion is offered at nearly the same amount. There is unfortuantely no way to measure the rates at which champions are actually offered currently, making this assumption neccessary.

Step 4 - Hypothesis Testing and Machine Learning¶

Now that we have the graphs and some idea of what the data looks like, we can try fitting models to them in hopes of more accurately identifying trends. We can plot linear regressions onto the scatter plots to see if they line up well with our assumptions.

An important note to keep in mind is that no matter what model we use, we will receieve a result. Simply getting a result from modelling does not guarantee it is a meaningful result. For example, the first graph we examined was Pick % vs Win %, to which we stated the data was quite noisy with no clear trend. We can still fit a model to this as follows...

Step 5 - Communication of Insights Attained¶

From The models we created, it would be difficult to say that a champion's Win %, Pick %, or KDA are dependant upon eachother in any fashion when it comes to ARAM exclusive statistics. Each model had large amounts of noise that, while a line was able to be fit, does not seem to produce trustowrthy results. This result was not altogether unexpected however. ARAM is, in reference to League of Legends as a whole, a secondary gamemode meant for players to take a break from the main game. It is treated as a more casual experience, and hardly ever receieves balance updates in comparison to Summoner's Rift. Players treat the mode as a place to mess around and have fun, often times losing as a result of a fun decision that costs them the game. The randomness plays a major role as well, as it often forces players into situations they are often unprepared for. Perhaps the lack of correlation is more telling than if a trend had been found.

As for the hypothesis on the performance of mages, the violin plots reveal that while mages can certainly perform well, they are hardly consistent. The bimodal plot of their win ratio revealed that mages seem to fall into two categories: the strong and the week. For a random gamemode, this means their overall performance as a class suffers. Since one of the Mage's "bulge" is the highest amongst the plotted values, my initial hypothesis was likely focused on the times I encountered this group of high-performance champions. Breaking down to Mage secondary roles further revealed some mages truly do suffer, with Mage/Support having the best consistency (evident by the majority of data being above %50 win rate. While some mages do perform well, it is difficult to say that the class as a whole has a significant statistical difference from the others. This would mean we cannot quite disprove the Null-Hypothesis as well as confidently confirm my own thoughts.

Where to go next?¶

With both hypotheses still in play, it may be worth it to investigate a new point of view with new data. I had mentioned earlier that champion difficulty may play a major role in this data as well. There certainly exists measures of champion difficulty, so they would need to be evaluated before starting again with a new dataframe. We also did not make use of the "Trend" column, which offers insight into how the champion is performing in reference to the previous patch. It could be worth exploring individual champions performance over multiple patches to see if we could predict their future abilities.

In regards to modeling and practice, I would recommend trying from a more consistent environment. I chose ARAM as my base of data as I thought the randomness would have some interesting effects on data (with which I am not disappointed). However, randomness often needs explaining and sometimes can never truly be pushed aside. A better environment for proving champion performance would certainly be the ranked format of League of Legends main gamemode, Summoner's Rift. The game is updated with this mode specifically as the focal point of design, so it would likely yield some interesting trends. One could also break apart the gamemode into its individual ranks, and see if performance varies via ranking (Bronze tier players may be worse than Platinum tier players at certain champions.
